Ethical Analysis of Facial Recognition

Examining FRT through different ethical frameworks reveals competing values and priorities in its development and deployment.

Rights-Based Ethics

Core Question: Does facial recognition violate fundamental human rights?

Key Considerations:

  • Right to Privacy: FRT enables constant identification in public spaces, eliminating traditional expectations of anonymity.
  • Freedom of Assembly: Surveillance may deter participation in protests or political gatherings.
  • Due Process: False identifications can lead to wrongful detentions without proper recourse.

Implications: From this perspective, FRT often violates privacy rights that are essential for human dignity and democratic participation. Some scholars argue public FRT use should be banned unless strict safeguards are implemented.

Consequentialism

Core Question: Do the benefits of FRT outweigh its harms?

Potential Benefits:

  • Faster identification of criminal suspects
  • Finding missing persons
  • Preventing terrorist attacks
  • Streamlining security processes

Potential Harms:

  • False arrests and wrongful convictions
  • Reinforcement of systemic biases
  • Normalization of mass surveillance
  • Potential for authoritarian abuse

Implications: This calculus varies by context. While FRT may provide security benefits, current implementations often disproportionately harm marginalized groups. Strict accuracy requirements and bias mitigation could alter the balance.

Additional Ethical Perspectives

Virtue Ethics

What kind of society do we become by normalizing constant surveillance? FRT may erode trust and social bonds by creating a panopticon effect where citizens assume they're always being watched.

Feminist Ethics

FRT often reflects and amplifies patriarchal structures. Systems trained primarily on male faces perform worse for women, and surveillance disproportionately impacts women's freedom in public spaces.

Critical Race Theory

FRT embeds racial hierarchies in code. The technology's origins in phrenology and its current disproportionate impact on communities of color reveal systemic racism in technological development.
